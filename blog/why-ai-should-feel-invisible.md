There's a pattern I keep seeing in AI products right now.

They make the AI the point. The chatbot, the assistant, the co-pilot — it's front and center, labeled and branded, impossible to ignore.

I think that's backwards.

## The goal is the outcome, not the tool

When I use Google Maps, I don't think about the routing algorithm. I just get where I'm going. The intelligence is there — deeply embedded — but it's invisible. It doesn't ask me to interact with it. It just works.

That's what AI should feel like.

When you're building a system that helps a warehouse manager track inventory, the AI shouldn't announce itself. It should just surface the right information at the right moment. The manager shouldn't have to learn a new interface — they should just notice that their job got easier.

## Why most AI products get this wrong

Building an invisible AI feature is harder than building a visible one.

A visible AI feature has clear UX patterns — there's a text box, you type, it responds. Simple to design, simple to explain, simple to demo.

An invisible AI feature requires you to deeply understand the workflow it's embedded in. Where are the friction points? What decisions does the user make repeatedly? What information do they wish they had but don't know to ask for?

That's hard. It requires time in the field, not just time at the keyboard.

## What I'm building toward

Every AI feature I build, I ask: could the user explain this without mentioning AI?

If yes — it's invisible. That's the goal.

If no — it means the AI is still the product, not the solution. Keep going.
