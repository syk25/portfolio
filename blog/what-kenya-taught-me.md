I spent time in Kenya working on a project that was supposed to help local farmers track market prices in real time.

We built it. It worked perfectly on our laptops.

Nobody used it.

## The gap between solution and context

The farmers we were trying to help didn't have consistent internet access. The phones they used were older models with limited storage. And most importantly — they trusted word of mouth from other farmers far more than an app built by strangers.

We had built the right solution for the wrong context.

## What I carry with me now

Every time I start a project now, I ask three questions before writing a single line of code:

1. **Who is actually going to use this?** Not the person who commissioned it — the person who has to live with it.
2. **What does their day look like?** What are they doing before and after they touch this software?
3. **What does success look like to them?** Not to me, not to the client — to them.

These questions slow down the start. But they almost always speed up the finish.

## Technology should feel invisible

The best software I've ever used, I barely remember using. It just... worked. It got out of the way and let me do what I came to do.

That's what I'm building toward — software that disappears into people's lives in the best possible way.
